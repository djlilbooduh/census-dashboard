# US Presidential Election Voting Data by Race and Political Party
## 2016 · 2020 · 2024

**Compiled for school project — May 2026**

---

## Executive Summary

This report compiles voting patterns by race and political party across the last three US presidential elections (2016, 2020, 2024). The data comes from three primary sources: CNN/National Election Pool (NEP) exit polls, Pew Research Center validated voter studies, and the US Census Bureau's Current Population Survey Voting and Registration Supplement.

**The biggest story across these elections is the dramatic realignment of Hispanic/Latino voters.** In 2016, Democrats held a 36-point advantage with Hispanic voters (65% to 29%). By 2024, that lead had collapsed to just 6 points (52% to 46%). Meanwhile, Black voters remain the most reliably Democratic group, White voters continue to lean Republican (though by shrinking margins), and Asian American voters showed significant movement toward Republicans in 2024.

**Key Takeaways:**

- Black voters gave Clinton her largest margin, at 88%.
- Third-party voting hit 5.7% nationally, the highest of the three elections, driven by Libertarian Gary Johnson and Green Party Jill Stein.
- Hispanic and Asian voting patterns were nearly identical in 2016 — both went 65% Democratic, 29% Republican.
- Trump won White voters by 21 points, which proved decisive in key swing states.

- Trump gained among Hispanic voters (29% → 32%), a preview of the larger shift to come in 2024.

## Cross-Election Trends

### 1. The Hispanic/Latino Realignment

This is the single biggest story in recent American electoral politics. Democratic support among Hispanic voters dropped by 13 percentage points from 2016 to 2024 (65% → 52%), while Republican support rose by 17 points (29% → 46%). 

**Driving factors include:**
- Economic concerns and inflation (consistently top issue for Hispanic voters)
- Growing working-class Hispanic identification with the Republican Party
- Regional shifts, especially in South Florida (Cuban/Venezuelan Americans) and South Texas (Rio Grande Valley)
- A widening education gap among Hispanic voters (college-educated more Democratic, non-college more Republican)
- Hispanic men shifted more dramatically than Hispanic women

### 2. Black Voter Stability — With Cracks

Black voters remain overwhelmingly Democratic, but the 2024 election showed meaningful erosion. Trump's 13% share among Black voters in 2024 was up from 8% in 2016. This shift was most pronounced among:
- Black men (especially younger Black men)
- Black voters without a college degree
- Black voters in key swing states

Despite this, Black voters still gave Democrats their highest support of any racial group (85% in 2024).

### 3. White Voters — Gradual Change

The White electorate has barely budged in terms of party preference: Trump won White voters by 21 points in 2016, 17 points in 2020, and 16 points in 2024. However, beneath the surface:
- College-educated White voters have shifted toward Democrats
- Non-college White voters remain strongly Republican (Trump won them by ~30 points)
- White women have become more competitive (Trump won White women 53%-46% in 2020)
- The White share of the electorate dipped in 2020 but returned to 71% in 2024

### 4. Asian American Voters — Shifting Right

Asian American voters, the fastest-growing racial group in the electorate, have shifted notably toward Republicans. Democratic support fell from 65% (2016) to 54% (2024), while Republican support rose from 29% to 39%. The 7% third-party vote share for Asians in 2024 was the highest among all racial groups — more than double the national average.

### 5. Turnout Trends

| Year | Overall Turnout | White | Black | Hispanic | Asian |
|------|-----------------|-------|-------|----------|-------|
| 2016 | 61.4% | 65.3% | 59.6% | 47.6% | 49.3% |
| 2020 | 66.8% | 70.9% | 62.6% | 53.7% | 59.7% |
| 2024 | 63.9% | 67.2% | 64.1% | 50.5% | 59.2% |

The 2020 turnout spike was historic — the highest since 1992 — and was driven by pandemic-era expansions of mail and early voting. Turnout fell back in 2024 across all groups but remained above 2016 levels for Black and Asian voters. Notably, **Black turnout in 2024 was actually higher than in 2016** (64.1% vs. 59.6%), even as overall turnout declined.

## Important Methodological Notes

### Exit Poll vs. Census vs. Validated Voter Data

Three different methodologies produce three different pictures:

1. **Exit Polls (CNN/NEP):** Survey voters as they leave polling places (plus phone surveys for early/absentee voters). Good for understanding *who voted for whom*, but subject to sampling error and non-response bias. Margins of error range from ±2 points for large groups to ±5-8 points for smaller subgroups.

2. **Census CPS Voting Supplement:** Large-sample household survey that asks about registration and voting *after* each election. Best source for turnout and registration rates by demographic group. However, it relies on self-reported voting, which tends to overstate turnout (social desirability bias).

3. **Pew Validated Voter Studies:** Matches survey respondents to commercial voter files to confirm actual turnout. More accurate than self-reports but limited to registered voters and depends on the quality of voter file matching.

### Why the Numbers May Differ Across Sources

- Exit polls measure Election Day + early voters; validated voter data covers the full electorate.
- Different methodologies classify race/ethnicity differently (e.g., exit polls use interviewer observation, surveys use self-identification).
- "Hispanic/Latino" is an ethnicity, not a race — Census data separates race and Hispanic origin, while exit polls often combine them into mutually exclusive categories.
- Small sample sizes for Asian and "Other" categories mean those numbers have larger margins of error.
